# Negative Space Checklist — What to DELETE

After drafting, delete every sentence matching these patterns.
Test: remove the sentence. If the argument still works, it was unnecessary.

Source: forensic analysis of what Ou-Wang-Yao (2007) deliberately omits.

## 10 Categories Ou Never Writes

| # | Category | Example | Why |
|---|----------|---------|-----|
| 1 | Preview sentences (self-promotional) | "The key innovation is..." | Don't evaluate your own work |
| 2 | Gap narrative | "Despite extensive literature..." | Let reader judge the gap |
| 3 | Self-evaluation of results | "This result is surprising..." | Don't evaluate your own results |
| 4 | Self-evaluation of method | "The key innovation is..." | Don't evaluate your own method |
| 5 | "Note that" for emphasis | "It is worth noting that..." | All sentences have equal weight |
| 6 | Restatements | "In other words..." | First statement is precise |
| 7 | Examples | "For example..." | Entire paper has zero examples |
| 8 | "As follows:" | "as follows:" | Reader can see what follows |
| 9 | Inline cross-references | "see [1]", "cf. §2" | References go in bibliography |
| 10 | Hence/Therefore/Consequently | — | Only Thus and So |

**Note on category 1 (2026-07-11)**: Ou DOES write functional transitions like
"Before giving the main result, we introduce some preliminary notations"
and "This paper is organized as follows." These are NOT self-promotional
preview sentences — they serve a navigational purpose. The prohibition is
against sentences that evaluate the work ("key", "central", "crucial").

Ou also uses "It is clear that" once (L58 of the published paper) — this
is a minor violation of the "no clearly/obviously" rule in his own paper.
Do not use this as a precedent; the published text is not immune to small
imperfections.

After finishing a paragraph, delete each sentence one by one.
If the argument remains complete without it, the sentence stays deleted.
